Research

Doctoral Project

My main field of research is astroparticle physics, with a focus on radio emissions from cosmic ray air showers. This field of research currently enjoys a surge of attention due to its cost-effectiveness, enabling the construction of detectors spanning vast geographical regions. This expanded detector coverage yields a higher particle detection rate within shorter time frames.

Until now, radio measurements have often been combined with other detector types. The experiment I am currently involved in (GRAND) pursues a purely radio-based approach, a pioneering endeavor that has not yet been realized at the desired scale. This novel direction introduces numerous technical and analytical challenges that demand innovative solutions. GRAND’s ultimate goal envisions deploying 200,000 antennas capable of nano-second resolution measurements. Given the impracticality of continuously storing data due to the astronomical volumes it would entail, my responsibility centers on devising a trigger mechanism to selectively capture and record actual air shower events, ensuring efficient data management and meaningful analysis.

Astroparticle Physics

Astroparticle physics bridges the gap between particle physics and cosmology, linking the behavior of subatomic particles to the large-scale structure and dynamics of the Universe. This interdisciplinary approach allows scientists to address questions that span multiple scales, leading to a more comprehensive and unified picture of the cosmos. It contributes to our understanding of the Universe, the fundamental nature of matter and energy, and advances the development of innovative technologies.

Investigating the radio signals generated during the interaction of cosmic rays with Earth's atmosphere helps us understand the fundamental properties of these cosmic messengers. We can study the origin, composition, and acceleration mechanisms of the most energetic particles. This research not only enhances our understanding of high-energy particle physics but also serves as a probe of distant astrophysical sources, such as supernovae and active galactic nuclei, enabling us to indirectly explore their nature and behavior.

Moreover, the development of sensitive radio detectors for astroparticle studies drives technological innovation, benefiting both fundamental research and broader applications in communication, remote sensing, and medical imaging.
